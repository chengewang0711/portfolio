01
The Problem Background

Firefighter training is essential for high-risk response — yet real-environment, live-fire training is costly, hard to scale, and lacks safe, repeatable, effective methodology.

Real environmentsAuthentic stress, but costly, dangerous and impossible to repeat at scale.
VR-onlySafe and repeatable, but no heat, no weight, no physical pressure.
By the numbers

Training is already one of the largest sources of firefighter injury — and most of those injuries are physical strain, the very load a screen-only simulator can’t deliver.

02
Why It Matters Pain Point

VR-only systems can't simulate the sensory load of a real fire — temperature, weight, physical pressure. The result is inadequate training that risks injury and resists accurate assessment.

P-01 · Safety risks

Live-fire drills expose trainees to burns, smoke inhalation and collapse hazards.

P-02 · Difficult to evaluate

Outcomes are judged by eye on paper — subjective, inconsistent and hard to compare.

P-03 · Lack of realism

Pure VR misses heat, load and pressure — the body never learns the real stress.

P-01 · Safety risks

03
The Approach Ideation

Leveraging physical feedback and data-driven assessment, the system delivers immersive training in real buildings without live fire. Rather than replicating the fire itself, it simulates the physical and psychological stress the fire provokes.

Four stages take a trainee from briefing to after-action report — fully repeatable, fully measured.

Firefighters put on the integrated MR system while instructors sync mission-specific tasks to each trainee. After selecting the teaching mode, trainees preview the fire location and operation map before entry.

02

Dynamic scene overlay

On entering the training area, the system overlays a dynamic fire scenario onto the physical space — generated from the building's structural blueprint. Drag the divider to see the same room transform.

TouchTune · Tactile Music Learning

TouchTune

A music learning device for blind children

Sheet music, instrument and audio feedback in one warm, tactile loop — recognize notes in braille, practice them on a speaking keyboard, then compose and record your own.

Recognize Practice Compose
Scroll
In motion

01 — THE GAP

Sheet music assumes you can see it.

Blind children who want to learn music face a broken loop: braille scores are printed, bulky and silent; instruments give sound but no notation; lessons depend on a teacher's eyes. TouchTune closes the loop with one device — read it, play it, write it — through touch and sound alone.

Verbal instruction
Teacher-dependentLimited autonomy
Paper braille scores
Low portabilityHigh learning burden
✕
Braille staff notation

Complex structure — difficult to memorize through touch.

✓
Braille numbered notation

TouchTune's choice — compact, countable, learnable by fingertip.

Source: Braille Authority of North America, Music Braille Code, 2015
STEP 1
Recognize notes

A refreshable braille sheet renders any score as raised, touchable notes — page by page, with numbered notation.

STEP 2
Practice with keys

Fifteen braille-embossed keys speak every note aloud. The sheet raises the next note; fingers find it and confirm.

STEP 3
Compose & record

Record turns playing into writing: every key pressed becomes braille on the sheet — a score the child authored.

02 — THE HARDWARE

Every control answers three questions: touch, output, next.

Soft white body, tactile grey keys, and a single vivid orange cue for the active action. Tap the orange dots to explore each zone.

Signature control

Key Control knob

The single vivid orange control. Turning it transposes everything you play into a new musical key.

03 — KEY DETAILS

Three details carry the experience.

Key Control knob

Satin orange, edge-knurled, placed at the corner where a thumb naturally rests. One turn — one new key.

Refreshable braille sheet

Three rows of cells with page chevrons and an embossed page number — a score that re-renders itself.

Electronic braille module

Each cell is a micro-actuator stack: six pins rise and fall to form any note, thousands of times over.

Oxy Loop

A plateau-circulating shared oxygen service

Reusable oxygen for high-altitude travel — rented, refilled, recycled and rewarded at public stations, so a single breath never becomes single-use waste.

Scroll
In motion

See the loop in action.

01 — Background

At altitude, a single breath leaves a lasting trace.

High-altitude travel runs on single-use oxygen cans. Each climber empties several, then discards them — turning remote, fragile plateaus into fields of metal litter that cannot easily be carried back out.

Resource waste

Disposable canisters are used once and thrown away — manufacturing and metal lost with every breath.

Plateau litter

At altitude there is no collection. Empty cans accumulate where they fall, scarring protected environments.

1×
Each can is used once, then discarded at altitude
0
Collection or recycling on the mountain today
Metal
Non-degradable litter left on fragile terrain
The idea

Turn a disposable product into a circular service.

Instead of selling cans, Oxy Loop runs public stations where one reusable canister keeps moving through four steps — never leaving the loop, never leaving the mountain.

02 — Product

A reusable canister built to be refilled, not thrown away.

Translucent, light, and durable — the Oxy Loop canister carries the same friendly oxygen clarity as the brand, with a recoverable mask and a refill valve designed for station service.

Refillable core

An internal pressure vessel designed to be topped up at the station — the body stays in service for life.

Recoverable mask

The mask detaches and drops into the recycling chute — later remade into souvenirs.

Station kiosk

Public Rent · Refill · Recycle bays with large tap targets for quick use at altitude.
